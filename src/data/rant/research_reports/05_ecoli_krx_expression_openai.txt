# E. coli KRX strain – details and induction system  
**Manufacturer:** Promega (Single-Step KRX Competent Cells  ([www.promega.com](https://www.promega.com/products/protein-expression/cell-based-protein-expression/#:~:text=BL21,proteins%20that%20are%20toxic%20to))). KRX is an E. coli K-12 derivative engineered for cloning and expression ([www.promega.com](https://www.promega.com/es-es/resources/protocols/technical-bulletins/101/single-step-krx-competent-cells-protocol/#:~:text=Single%20Step%20,copies%20of%20the%20lacZ%20gene)). It has mutations (recA^–, endA^–, ΔhsdR17, Δe14, ompT^–, ompP^–) that boost plasmid stability and reduce proteolysis ([www.promega.com](https://www.promega.com/es-es/resources/protocols/technical-bulletins/101/single-step-krx-competent-cells-protocol/#:~:text=Single%20Step%20,copies%20of%20the%20lacZ%20gene)). It also carries lacZΔM15/F′ for blue–white screening.  

**Regulatory system:** KRX harbors a single chromosomal copy of T7 RNA polymerase under the rhaBAD (rhaP_BAD) promoter ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5624749/#:~:text=Using%20the%20T7%20RNA%20polymerase,ensuring%20a%20long%20induction%20phase)). In practice, cells are grown with glucose (repressing), then induced by adding L-rhamnose. The native rhamnose pathway genes are deleted in KRX, so rhamnose is not consumed, giving a long induction period ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5624749/#:~:text=Using%20the%20T7%20RNA%20polymerase,ensuring%20a%20long%20induction%20phase)). The rhaBAD promoter has very low basal activity (as a positively activated system), yielding essentially no expression until induction ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2254391/#:~:text=to%20express%20a%20variety%20of,large%20moiety%20of%20insoluble%20protein)). This provides much tighter control than the traditional IPTG-Lac system in BL21(DE3) ([www.promega.com](https://www.promega.com/products/protein-expression/cell-based-protein-expression/#:~:text=BL21,are%20recommended%20for%20expression%20of)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2254391/#:~:text=to%20express%20a%20variety%20of,large%20moiety%20of%20insoluble%20protein)). In fact, Promega notes that KRX exhibits dramatically lower background expression than BL21(DE3) strains ([www.promega.com](https://www.promega.com/products/protein-expression/cell-based-protein-expression/#:~:text=BL21,are%20recommended%20for%20expression%20of)), making it ideal for toxic proteins. The rhaP_BAD-T7 system requires the cAMP–CRP complex (catabolite activation) to fully induce ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2254391/#:~:text=plasmids.%20Therefore%2C%20only%20the%20rhaP_,is%20a%20key%20regulator%20of)), analogous to lac, and it is also repressed by glucose ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5624749/#:~:text=Using%20the%20T7%20RNA%20polymerase,ensuring%20a%20long%20induction%20phase)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2254391/#:~:text=to%20express%20a%20variety%20of,large%20moiety%20of%20insoluble%20protein)).  

**Expression yields:** KRX is reported to give high protein yields, in many cases comparable or superior to BL21(DE3). For example, genome sequencing of KRX notes that “high protein yields can be achieved” via the rhamnose-T7 system, and in some studies KRX outperformed other E. coli strains ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5624749/#:~:text=Using%20the%20T7%20RNA%20polymerase,ensuring%20a%20long%20induction%20phase)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5624749/#:~:text=Escherichia%20coli%20KRX%20is%20a,coli%20strains%20%28%2010)). In one comparison, KRX produced more active luciferase per unit absorbance in 4 h than 20 h induction in TB (promega short induction note ([www.promega.com](https://www.promega.com/resources/pubhub/enotes/protein-expression-in-less-time/#:~:text=An%20increase%20in%20Renilla%20luciferase,continuing%20through%20later%20time%20points))); Strains like KRX often reach mg-per-liter scale for well-expressed proteins (ProMab reports avg. ~3 mg/L ([www.promab.com](https://www.promab.com/e-coli-expression#:~:text=,Solubility%20is%20not%20guaranteed)), similar to many BL21 yields). Published reports of KRX vs. BL21 yields are sparse, but anecdotal reports (e.g. cytochrome P450, laccases, Argonaute) suggest KRX can match or exceed BL21(D22)/BL21(DE3) yields ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5624749/#:~:text=Escherichia%20coli%20KRX%20is%20a,coli%20strains%20%28%2010)).  

**96-well/HTP protocols:** While KRX is mainly documented in shake-flask or small flask formats, high-throughput 96-well protocols can use the same principles. A Promega note describes induction in LB (with 0.4% glucose) to OD~0.4–0.5, then shifting to 25 °C and adding 0.1% rhamnose ([www.promega.com](https://www.promega.com/resources/pubhub/enotes/protein-expression-in-less-time/#:~:text=Technical%20Bulletin%20,for%20gel%20analysis)). In that protocol, 4 h of induction yielded detectable bioluminescent protein, enabling same-day analysis ([www.promega.com](https://www.promega.com/resources/pubhub/enotes/protein-expression-in-less-time/#:~:text=An%20increase%20in%20Renilla%20luciferase,continuing%20through%20later%20time%20points)). This “short induction” protocol showed ~4 h at 25 °C yields more active protein per cell (OD) than overnight. We did not find a published 96-well plate protocol specifically, but one could adapt this (e.g. 0.1% rhamnose, 25 °C, 4–6 h) in small volumes ([www.promega.com](https://www.promega.com/resources/pubhub/enotes/protein-expression-in-less-time/#:~:text=Technical%20Bulletin%20,for%20gel%20analysis)) ([www.promega.com](https://www.promega.com/resources/pubhub/enotes/protein-expression-in-less-time/#:~:text=An%20increase%20in%20Renilla%20luciferase,continuing%20through%20later%20time%20points)). In summary, KRX’s rhamnose/T7 system works with standard T7 vectors and induction conditions (0.05–0.2% rhamnose, 25–30 °C, 2–16 h) and is documented to yield significant protein in hours ([www.promega.com](https://www.promega.com/resources/pubhub/enotes/protein-expression-in-less-time/#:~:text=An%20increase%20in%20Renilla%20luciferase,continuing%20through%20later%20time%20points)) ([www.neb.com](https://www.neb.com/en/applications/protein-expression/cell-free-protein-expression/nebexpress-cell-free-e-coli-protein-synthesis-system#:~:text=The%20NEBExpress%C2%AE%20Cell,control%20of%20T7%20RNA%20Polymerase)).  

# 2. Cell-free vs. cell-based E. coli expression  
**Yields:** Modern E. coli cell-free systems produce only µg-scale protein per reaction. For example, NEBExpress (E. coli lysate) yields up to ~0.5 mg/mL (≈25 µg in a 50 µL reaction), while PURE (PURExpress) yields ~0.25 mg/mL (≈12 µg/50 µL) ([www.neb.com](https://www.neb.com/en/faqs/are-the-yields-with-nebexpress-cell-free-e-coli-protein-synthesis-system-comparable-with-the-purexpress-in-vitro-protein-synthesis-kit#:~:text=nature%20of%20the%20target%20protein,of%20the%20template%20and%20supplements)). In contrast, even small-scale E. coli cultures (shake flasks or deep-well plates) achieve mg-scale yields. ProMab reports *0.5–50 mg per liter* (average ≈3 mg/L) from 1 L cultures ([www.promab.com](https://www.promab.com/e-coli-expression#:~:text=,Solubility%20is%20not%20guaranteed)). In other words, a 1 mL culture of E. coli can easily yield tens to hundreds of µg, far above a single 50 µL CFPS reaction. Many HTP screens obtain mg/L yields (routinely 0.1–100 mg/L range ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4692350/#:~:text=screening%20on%20a%20small%20scale,automated%20with%20the%20use%20of))). 

**Cost:** Cell-free reagents are expensive. A 100-reaction kit of NEBExpress (50 µL per reaction) costs on the order of \$1,700 (≈\$17 per reaction, i.e. ≈\$680 per mg of protein ([www.neb.com](https://www.neb.com/en/faqs/are-the-yields-with-nebexpress-cell-free-e-coli-protein-synthesis-system-comparable-with-the-purexpress-in-vitro-protein-synthesis-kit#:~:text=nature%20of%20the%20target%20protein,of%20the%20template%20and%20supplements))). Other vending systems (e.g. Promega TNT, PUREAG) are similar or higher. By contrast, E. coli culture media is very cheap: 1 L LB ~\$1, so 1 mL ~\$0.001, plus IPTG/rhamnose (cents per mL) and antibiotics (negligible). Thus ingredient cost for a 1 mL culture is on the order of a few cents, producing perhaps 3–10 mg. Even at 10 cents per well, that is <$25 per mg. The main costs of cell-based are labor and time. Our automated system would drive labor *essentially to zero* per run, making cell-based far cheaper per mg (aside from capital and overhead). 

**Turnaround:** Cell-free is fast. Reactions take just a few hours once DNA template is ready. NEB notes proteins can be synthesized and detected in ~2–4 h ([www.neb.com](https://www.neb.com/en/applications/protein-expression/cell-free-protein-expression/nebexpress-cell-free-e-coli-protein-synthesis-system#:~:text=The%20NEBExpress%C2%AE%20Cell,control%20of%20T7%20RNA%20Polymerase)). In practice, from purified DNA one can (in principle) have a “protein in hand” within the same day. E. coli expression requires cell growth and induction – typically a 16 h overnight induction after transform. Even with rapid protocols (like KRX’s 4–6 h induction ([www.promega.com](https://www.promega.com/resources/pubhub/enotes/protein-expression-in-less-time/#:~:text=An%20increase%20in%20Renilla%20luciferase,continuing%20through%20later%20time%20points))), the full cycle from plasmid to protein is usually ∼1–2 days. 

**Preferred scenarios:** *Cell-free is preferred* when speed or expression of difficult targets is needed. It excels for very rapid prototyping and toxic or insoluble proteins ([www.thermofisher.com](https://www.thermofisher.com/tr/en/home/technical-resources/technical-reference-library/protein-expression-support-center/cell-free-expression-support/cell-free-expression-support-getting-started.html#:~:text=A%20cell,expression%20in%20a%20single%20tube)). For example, Thermo’s FAQ highlights cell-free use cases: toxic proteins (no living cells needed), insoluble proteins (avoid inclusion bodies), protease-sensitive proteins, incorporation of nonstandard amino acids or labels, and straightforward high-throughput formatting ([www.thermofisher.com](https://www.thermofisher.com/tr/en/home/technical-resources/technical-reference-library/protein-expression-support-center/cell-free-expression-support/cell-free-expression-support-getting-started.html#:~:text=What%20are%20the%20advantages%20of,free%20expression%20system)). *E. coli expression is preferred* when larger yields (>100 µg) are needed at low cost, or when proteins fold well in bacteria. For routine antibody fragments or metabolic enzymes, E. coli is usually cheaper and scales easily. In summary, cell-free is great for rapid mg-or-less prototyping, but E. coli (especially automated 96-well KRX) wins for multi-sample screens needing higher protein amounts.

Genotype-to-phenotype pipelines usually use E. coli (to produce protein directly in wells). KRX could automate expression of moderate-sized libraries (hundreds of clones) with tight regulation (beneficial for toxic mutants) and any existing T7-based plasmids. In enzyme discovery or pathway engineering, researchers may test tens of enzyme variants in vitro, each requiring >10 µg protein for assays. Current workflows typically use shake flasks or 24/96-well deep plates with auto-induction. Our KRX service fits well: microbial enzymes usually fold correctly in E. coli, and mg/L yields are common. A cloud lab could deliver purified enzymes for hundreds of variants faster than in-house culturing. 

- **Antibody fragments (scFv, VHH) and custom binders:** Display technologies (phage, yeast) yield dozens of hits (scFv or VHH) to test from a campaign. Confirming these often requires ~0.5–1 mg per variant for binding assays (ELISA, SPR). E. coli (periplasmic) expression is standard for scFvs or VHHs, although correct disulfide formation is needed. KRX could be used with secretion signals for periplasmic folding. Throughput is moderate (10s of variants), making a 1–2 day express service attractive if costs are low. - **Protein–protein interaction (PPI) studies:** Mapping PPIs or screening peptides/proteins for binding (e.g. pull-downs or pulldowns, Yeast 2-hybrid follow-up) usually involves a relatively small number of proteins (<50). Each needs mg quantities for assays. E. coli KRX can produce many soluble bacterial or small eukaryotic domains quickly. For higher eukaryotic proteins requiring secretion or glycosylation, KRX is not ideal. Still, any PPI project needing ~1 mg per protein (e.g. biophysical/BLI assays) could use this service. Overall throughput needs are lower here, so value comes from low-labor automation rather than extreme scale.  

Each of these applications can benefit from our “next-day expression” model. In general, they involve *dozens to low hundreds* of variants (larger than what cell-free can economically handle) and require more protein per variant than rapid CFPS yields. E. coli KRX is compatible with many enzymes and non-glycosylated binders, so for these areas (especially AI-driven design and enzyme screening) our service is highly suitable.

These proteins typically are 1–100 kDa, soluble in bacteria, and require tens-of-µg quantities to assess function. An automated KRX pipeline (96-well induction with rhamnose) can produce these yields in a day at low labor cost. This aligns perfectly with our strategy: e.g. biotech/startups like Arzeda, Generate or EvolutionaryScale will generate new sequences and could send them in batches for expression ([techcrunch.com](https://techcrunch.com/2024/09/17/arzeda-is-using-ai-to-design-proteins-for-artificial-sweeteners-and-more/#:~:text=%E2%80%9CWe%20have%20developed%20filters%20based,%E2%80%9D)) ([www.axios.com](https://www.axios.com/2024/06/25/ai-biotech-generative-model-protein-design#:~:text=Zoom%20in%3A%20The%20team%20asked,scientist%20Alexander%20Rives%20told%20Axios)). 

- **Enzyme/biocatalyst libraries and panels.** Projects engineering enzymes (e.g. metabolic pathway enzymes, industrial biocatalysts) often screen ~10–100 variants. They need mg-scale, soluble protein for kinetics and stability tests. KRX is a good fit: bacterial and most fungal enzymes express well. Compared to cell-free, our approach yields much more protein per variant. These customers currently grow cultures manually; a faster automated rush service (analysis next day) would accelerate R&D. 

- **Antibody fragment/nanobody screening (to a lesser extent).** Although scFv and VHH require disulfide bonds, many labs use engineered E. coli (e.g. SHuffle or periplasmic expression) to get mg of binder quickly. If KRX is used with secretion signals, it could express such fragments overnight. This is attractive for antibody-discovery services that currently perform 1–10 clone expressions—if our service could handle dozens/day at moderate cost, it would be valuable. (However, standard bed-of-nails immunoglobulin binds often still use mammalian or yeast systems for final validation.)  

In conclusion, **applications involving moderate-throughput (10–100s) of bacterial protein variants needing >tens of µg each** stand out. These include ML-guided protein design campaigns and enzyme variant screening. In these scenarios, our automated KRX expression (turnaround ~24 h, cost ~0.5–1% of traditional CRO/kit costs) delivers clear value over both cell-free and manual bacterial expression.
