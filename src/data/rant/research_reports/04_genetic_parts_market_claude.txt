## 2. iGEM Registry of Standard Biological Parts

### Scale



The iGEM Parts Registry is a growing collection of genetic parts that can be mixed and matched to build synthetic biology devices and systems. As part of the synthetic biology community's efforts to make biology easier to engineer, it provides a source of genetic parts to iGEM teams.



The Registry contains approximately **20,000–30,000 registered parts** (based on historical counts; the exact current number varies as the migration to the new registry platform continues). 

Most of the core part data (sequences, descriptions, and metadata) has been copied over to the new modern Registry at registry.igem.org.



### Quality: "Works Reliably" vs. Theoretical

This is a critical issue. A 2021 curation study identified major quality challenges: 

While there are a number of large community repositories of design information, curation of this information has been limited. This in turn limits the ways in which design information can be put to use. The study analyzed the SBOL version of the iGEM registry using four different approaches. Key challenges encountered include the use of free text, insufficient part provenance, part duplication, lack of part removal, and insufficient continuous curation.



**Realistic estimate**: Of the ~20,000+ parts, probably only **1,000–2,000** have been experimentally validated with published characterization data. Many parts are duplicates, poorly documented, or simply sequence entries uploaded by student teams that were never tested beyond a single iGEM project. The vast majority are "theoretical" — a sequence exists but no reliable functional data accompanies it.

### Assembly Standards



Unless otherwise specified, most parts in the Registry comply with the original BioBrick assembly standard (also known as Assembly standard 10).



BioBrick (RFC 10) uses traditional restriction/ligation with EcoRI/XbaI/SpeI/PstI flanking each part — a simple but inherently limited standard (scar sequences between parts, sequential assembly only). The field has largely moved toward:

- **Golden Gate / MoClo**: Type IIS restriction enzymes (BsaI, BbsI) enabling scarless, multi-part, one-pot assembly. This is now the de facto standard for modular assembly.
- **Gibson Assembly**: Overlap-based, flexible but less standardized.

The 2024 iGEM distribution shows the shift: 

The iGEM 2024 distribution builds upon the 2023 one. It includes expansions to the Open Yeast collection and the iGEM Engineering Committee collections. They partnered with Asimov to manufacture a distribution of the highest quality, bringing in their best manufacturing practices, new automation workflows, and high quality control check points.





All Registry content falls under Creative Commons Attribution-ShareAlike.

 This is important — it's open but the ShareAlike clause has implications for commercial use.

### Key Takeaway for Genetic Assemblies
The iGEM Registry is large in number but poor in quality. It's a cautionary tale: a parts library without rigorous curation and functional validation has limited practical utility. This is precisely the gap Genetic Assemblies could fill.

## 4. The Open-Source Angle

### What Fraction is Freely Available?

- **Addgene**: All deposits are shared under institution-to-institution MTAs. 

Addgene employs legal and administrative staff who work with technology transfer offices (TTOs) to arrange completion of a material transfer agreement (MTA) for each plasmid exchange. An MTA is a legal contract governing the sharing of research materials between two organizations. The MTA defines the rights of both the provider and the recipient with respect to the materials.

 These MTAs typically restrict commercial use and redistribution.

- **iGEM Registry**: 

All Registry content falls under Creative Commons Attribution-ShareAlike.

 The CC-BY-SA license allows commercial use but requires derivative works to use the same license — a "copyleft" provision that some companies find restrictive.

- **Commercial kits** (NEB, Thermo): Proprietary, no open licenses.

### Licensing Restrictions — The Problem

**Addgene MTAs**: Most Addgene plasmids come under the UBMTA (Uniform Biological Material Transfer Agreement) or institution-specific MTAs. These typically:
- Prohibit redistribution to third parties
- Restrict commercial use without separate license
- Are signed between institutions, not individuals

This means: **A startup cannot legally take Addgene plasmids and incorporate them into a commercial product/service without negotiating separate commercial licenses with each depositor's institution.** This is a major barrier.

### Is There Demand for a Fully Open-Source, Commercially-Usable Parts Library?

**Yes, but the demand is latent and the market is small in direct revenue terms.** The pain points are:

1. **Startups** building engineered organisms need freedom-to-operate. MTA-encumbered parts from Addgene create IP uncertainty.
2. **Biofoundries and cloud labs** need redistributable, unencumbered DNA.
3. **iGEM-to-startup pipeline**: Teams that build iGEM projects and want to commercialize find BioBrick parts poorly characterized and CC-BY-SA creates complications.
4. **Community labs / DIYbio**: Want fully open tools.

The closest precedent is the **Open Enzyme Collection** and various "free genes" projects, but none have achieved critical mass as a commercially viable, well-curated library.

## 6. The Gap: Curated, Sequence-Verified, Ready-to-Use Parts

### The Quality Problem

Most Addgene plasmids are deposited by academic labs with:
- Partial sequencing (insert-only Sanger, not full plasmid)
- No standardized functional validation
- Variable documentation quality

The cost of consumable reagents to make a plasmid was calculated at $115/plasmid, excluding restriction enzymes or vector purchases. Restriction enzymes and vector purchases can raise the cost anywhere from a few dollars to over a hundred.

 Despite this, 

it takes five hours of hands-on time to make a single plasmid, based on simple insertion of a PCR product into a vector. The time it takes could increase dramatically if you are piecing together several PCR products. And this assumes you did everything correctly the first time – no mistakes, no repeats!



The iGEM Registry quality problem was documented systematically: 

Key challenges include the use of free text, insufficient part provenance, part duplication, lack of part removal, and insufficient continuous curation.



### Is There a Market for Professionally Curated Parts?

**Yes, but it's a wedge strategy, not a standalone business.** The value proposition is:

1. **For academics**: Every plasmid is full-length sequence-verified, functionally validated, and ships as ready-to-use DNA (not a stab you need to grow up). Worth a premium over Addgene's $85.

2. **For startups/industry**: Parts come with no MTA restrictions, full commercial use rights, and documented performance data. This is the real differentiator — freedom-to-operate.

3. **For the Genetic Assemblies funnel**: A free/open parts library drives traffic → users discover they need custom constructs → they order gene synthesis/assembly services (Stage 1 & 2 revenue).

### The Strategic Play

Building the largest open-source genetic toolkit works as:
- **Marketing & brand**: Establishes credibility as "the source" for modular DNA
- **Customer acquisition**: Free parts → paid assembly services
- **Lock-in via ecosystem**: Users design around your part standards, then need your assembly service
- **Data flywheel**: Usage data on which parts work tells you what custom orders will follow

The direct revenue from parts sales may be **$0–500K/year** in the early years. The indirect revenue from funneling users into $30/gene synthesis services is where the business case lives. If even 1% of Addgene's ~170K annual plasmid requesters convert to ordering custom genes at $30 each with a few genes per order, that's ~$150K–500K/year — from one channel alone.
