More recently, leading providers quote on the order of **7–10¢/bp** for large orders of gene fragments or clonal genes. (Prices vary by provider, length, quantity and QC.)  Overall, the trajectory has been roughly a tenfold price drop each 3–5 years: from cents in 2010 to pennies by mid‐2020s.

# Technology Transitions  
DNA synthesis chemistry has evolved from classic **column-based phosphoramidite** (one base at a time on resin/plastic) to **chip-based microarray synthesis**, and now to nascent **enzymatic methods**.  Column synthesis (1970s-’90s) yielded high-fidelity but was relatively low-throughput.  In the 2000s, array/chip methods (Agilent, CustomArray, Twist silicon chips) massively parallelized oligo production, driving costs down by ~10×.  Chip synthesis provides many short oligos cheaply, but requires downstream assembly for >200 nt lengths.  Most industry gene-writing thus uses chips for oligo pools plus ligation/PCR assembly.  Now, **enzymatic DNA synthesis** (template-free polymerization) is emerging.  Companies have developed modified terminal transferases and templated ligases to polymerize DNA with iterative base addition.  These promise longer contiguous synthesis with fewer toxic reagents; pilots and benchtop “DNA printers” are in development.  

Each step change impacted cost/quality: column synthesis had high per-base reagent cost but high fidelity per cycle; chip methods slashed per-base cost (e.g. Twist’s silicon chips) at the expense of needing error-correction; enzymatic approaches aim to combine low cost with high accuracy.  For example, one review notes that the >200 bp gene regime is still expensive, driving innovations like template-independent enzymatic synthesis and microarray-based methods ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9869848/#:~:text=editing%20technologies%2C%20DNA%20synthesis%20is,a%20whole%20and%20give%20an)).  

# Future Directions  
The industry is moving toward **enzymatic platforms, longer constructs and faster service**.  Leading trends include: 

- **Enzymatic DNA synthesis**.  Firms like **DNA Script**, **Nuclera Nucleics**, **Molecular Assemblies** (Benchtop TdT-based “DNA printers”) are commercializing template-free DNA polymerization ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9869848/#:~:text=thermostable%20TdT,fidelity%20enzymes%20to)).  Evonetix is developing thermal‐controlled microfluidic chips that “print” DNA on demand ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9869848/#:~:text=may%20be%20amplified%20by%20PCR,52)).  (DNA Script’s commercially available instrument can produce ~60 bp oligos in 6 hr, for instance.) These platforms tout low-cost, high-fidelity synthesis without traditional phosphoramidites.  

- **Longer reads / integrated assembly**.  Companies are improving the synthesis of multi-kilobase constructs via hierarchical assembly.  Major players (Twist, GenScript, IDT) now routinely offer fully sequence-verified genes up to several kilobases, and some specialty firms (e.g. BlueHeron Bio, Codex DNA) assemble genomes via Gibson/Gibson-like methods.  Research “gene foundries” and startups (Ginkgo Bio, Synthego, etc.) are engineering pipelines to write mega-base circuits.  

- **Faster turnaround & automation**.  High-throughput robotic “cloud labs” are compressing delivery times.  For example, companies like International DNA Technologies (IDT) and Twist offer 24–48 hr turnaround for many genes, and Azenta’s “Express” GeneWiz service offers ~3-day delivery ([www.biospace.com](https://www.biospace.com/azenta-reports-fourth-quarter-and-full-year-fiscal-2023-results-ended-september-30-2023#:~:text=,)).  Emerging offerings include desktop DNA synthesis machines that an end-user can run (Evonetix’s printer) and one-day on-site gene printers under development.  

In each trend, leaders are evident: **DNA Script** (enzymatic consumer systems), **Evonetix** (chip/‘printer’), **Ansa Biotechnologies** (enzyme engineering), and **Camena Bioscience** (high-fidelity enzymes) for enzymatic synthesis; and **Twist**, **GenScript**, **IDT/Danaher** in scaling up length and speed.  Overall, the market is shifting toward enzyme-based chemistry, longer continuous sequences and near-immediate “digital-to-DNA” synthesis, backed by AI-driven design and logistics.
