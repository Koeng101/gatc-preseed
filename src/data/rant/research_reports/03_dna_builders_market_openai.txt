# DIY Workflow & Per-Plasmid Costs  
Typical do-it-yourself cloning proceeds in steps.  A plausible workflow (for a ~0.5–1.2 kb insert) is:  
- **Order gene fragments (or oligos):** e.g. 1×1.2 kb gBlock or several smaller fragments.  Double-stranded gene fragments cost on the order of $0.25–0.30 per base (so about $250–$300 for a 1,000 bp fragment with IDT’s gBlocks™) ([www.idtdna.com](https://www.idtdna.com/pages/products/genes-and-gene-fragments/double-stranded-dna-fragments/gblocks-gene-fragments#:~:text=gBlocks%20Gene%20Fragments%20are%20double,developed%20for%20our%20Ultramer%E2%84%A2%C2%A0DNA%20Oligos)) or similar from Twist/GenScript.  
- **Prepare fragments:** PCR-amplify or digest as needed. Taq/HiFi polymerase reagents ~$2–$5, primers ~$10+.  
- **Assembly reaction:** Golden Gate or Gibson.  Cost of enzymes is modest: a Gibson master mix (NEB) is ~$3–4/reaction for typical 25–50 µL, or Golden Gate mix (BsaI + T4 ligase) similarly ~$2–3 if metered to single reaction ([blog.addgene.org](https://blog.addgene.org/the-time-and-cost-required-to-make-a-plasmid#:~:text=Doench%20also%20calculated%20the%20cost,dollars%20to%20over%20a%20hundred)).  
- **Transformation, plating, colony picking:** Transform 2–5 µL into competent cells, plate on antibiotic plates.  Hands-on time ~10–20 min, then 16–18 h incubation.  Picking ~5–10 colonies takes another 5–15 min.  
- **Miniprep:** Grow picked colony in 2–3 mL, spin down, purify plasmid (e.g. Qiagen spin kit ~$3–$5/sample).  
- **Sequence verification:** Send plasmid DNA for sequencing.  Traditional Sanger (primer walking) might cost ~$10–$20 for 4–5 reads (~1 kb each), while Plasmidsaurus charges $15 for full-length (＋$5 if using a colony directly) ([plasmidsaurus.com](https://plasmidsaurus.com/plasmid_sequencing#:~:text=Service%20Level%20%20,Column%20or)) ([plasmidsaurus.com](https://plasmidsaurus.com/plasmid_sequencing#:~:text=Service%20Level%20%20,in%2010%20%C2%B5L%20of%20water)).  

Putting it together, **consumable cost** per clone is roughly: $100–300 (fragments) + $5–15 (assembly enzymes) + $3–5 (miniprep) + ~$15 (sequencing) ≈ **$120–330** (depending on fragment cost and sequencing method).  Addgene estimates reagents alone were ~$115 (2018) before inflation ([blog.addgene.org](https://blog.addgene.org/the-time-and-cost-required-to-make-a-plasmid#:~:text=Doench%20also%20calculated%20the%20cost,dollars%20to%20over%20a%20hundred)); adjusting for prices today gives on the order of **$150–$200 in consumables**.  “Low-tech” kits (homemade competent cells, crude preps) can shave $20–30, but commercial-grade PCR/enzymes and kits cost more.  

In terms of **time**, Addgene reports ∼5 hours hands-on for a simple plasmid ([blog.addgene.org](https://blog.addgene.org/the-time-and-cost-required-to-make-a-plasmid#:~:text=According%20to%20his%20math%2C%20it,assumptions%20outlined%20in%20Table%201)) (per Doench’s calculation) and most people take **>22** hours total (spread over ~3–4 days) to get a clone ([blog.addgene.org](https://blog.addgene.org/the-time-and-cost-required-to-make-a-plasmid#:~:text=How%20long%20does%20it%20take,better%20part%20of%20four%20days)).  This includes designing primers, waiting on PCR/ligation, overnight growth, etc.  In practice, one often needs to screen several colonies: after plating a ligation, 5–10 colonies may be tested to find the correct construct.  One study found that for a 5-part Golden Gate assembly, picking 10 white (presumed-correct) colonies yielded 10/10 correct, but efficiency falls as part count grows ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6819997/#:~:text=Next%2C%20we%20investigated%20the%20process,shown%20in%20literature%20to%20impact)).  In summary, DIY cloning typically costs on the order of a few hundred dollars per plasmid plus ~0.5–1 day of a technician/postdoc’s time for a straightforward case ([blog.addgene.org](https://blog.addgene.org/the-time-and-cost-required-to-make-a-plasmid#:~:text=Doench%20also%20calculated%20the%20cost,dollars%20to%20over%20a%20hundred)) ([blog.addgene.org](https://blog.addgene.org/the-time-and-cost-required-to-make-a-plasmid#:~:text=According%20to%20his%20math%2C%20it,assumptions%20outlined%20in%20Table%201)).

# Assembly Method Adoption (Golden Gate vs Gibson vs Traditional)  
Among lab assembly techniques, **Gibson (or similar homology-based cloning)** has been the most common.  A 2013 synthetic biology survey found Gibson used by ~48% of respondents (current use) vs Golden Gate by ~16% ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3684516/#:~:text=physical%20assembly%20standards%20and%20methods,physical%20assembly%20methods%2C%20including%20the)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3684516/#:~:text=lower%20overall%20usage%20rates%20for,past%29%2C%20and%20others)).  In contrast, only ~18% reported using the old BioBrick standard, and Gateway~15% ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3684516/#:~:text=previously%20have%20used%20the%20Gibson,physical%20assembly%20methods%2C%20including%20the)).  This trend still holds: most molecular biology labs use Gibson/In-Fusion or even simple restriction/ligation for one-off cloning.  Golden Gate (type IIS) and MoClo standards have **grown popular in certain communities** (e.g. plant labs with Phytobricks, or metabolic engineering with modular parts), but they’re not yet universal.  One recent review notes that adopting modular standards *enhances reproducibility*, but no single MoClo standard exists for all organisms ([pubs.acs.org](https://pubs.acs.org/doi/10.1021/acssynbio.2c00355#:~:text=advanced%20DNA%20assembly%20tools,of%20a%20suite%20of%20parts)) – different groups use different “syntaxes.”  In practice, many labs adopt a whatever-the-lab-uses method: a lab with a Golden Gate repository (MoClo/GoldenBraid) will keep using that, while others stick to Gibson.  

- *Percentages:*  Gibson ~50% of labs, Golden Gate ~15–20% (per survey) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3684516/#:~:text=physical%20assembly%20standards%20and%20methods,physical%20assembly%20methods%2C%20including%20the)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3684516/#:~:text=lower%20overall%20usage%20rates%20for,past%29%2C%20and%20others)).  Traditional restriction cloning and other methods fill the rest.  
- *Standards:*  BioBrick-type standards (e.g. iGEM-style, PhytoBrick) have some community uptake, but still far from universal ([pubs.acs.org](https://pubs.acs.org/doi/10.1021/acssynbio.2c00355#:~:text=advanced%20DNA%20assembly%20tools,of%20a%20suite%20of%20parts)). Some hubs (e.g. BioBricks or Addgene collections) ease sharing, but most everyday projects do custom assembly (often with Gibson) unless a specific standardized vector library is in place.  

# Pain Points (Failure Rates & Labor Cost)  
DIY cloning is famously **error-prone and labor-intensive**.  Common pain points include:  
- **Multiple failure modes:**  PCR may fail (no insert), digests may incompletely cut, ligations/transforms may yield no colonies, or colonies might all be empty or incorrect.  As one guide put it: *“Cloning can be quite an arduous process… There’s a lot that can go wrong!”* ([blog.addgene.org](https://blog.addgene.org/plasmid-cloning-troubleshooting-guide#:~:text=Cloning%20can%20be%20quite%20an,plasmid%20with%20no%20substantial%20delays)).  Troubleshooting (re-designing primers, redoing PCRs, testing enzymes, etc.) often eats days.  
- **Screening burden:** Each assembly typically requires picking several colonies.  For example, researchers often screen 5–10 colonies to find one correct clone.  In one automated 5-part Golden Gate test, 10 white (presumed-correct) colonies were sequenced – all 10 were correct ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6819997/#:~:text=Next%2C%20we%20investigated%20the%20process,shown%20in%20literature%20to%20impact)), but that success came after optimizing conditions.  In general, success rate declines for larger/complex assemblies: e.g. assembling 8 fragments gave far fewer white colonies than a 2-fragment construct ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6819997/#:~:text=Next%2C%20we%20investigated%20the%20process,shown%20in%20literature%20to%20impact)), meaning more repeats and screening needed.  
- **Time:**  Addgene quotes ~5 h of hands-on work per plasmid (when everything goes smoothly) ([blog.addgene.org](https://blog.addgene.org/the-time-and-cost-required-to-make-a-plasmid#:~:text=According%20to%20his%20math%2C%20it,assumptions%20outlined%20in%20Table%201)), but in reality most people take >20 h (spread over 3–4 calendar days) to get a verified clone ([blog.addgene.org](https://blog.addgene.org/the-time-and-cost-required-to-make-a-plasmid#:~:text=How%20long%20does%20it%20take,better%20part%20of%20four%20days)).  Almost every day of that may involve waiting (PCR runs or overnight cultures). Repeated failures extend this further.  
- **Opportunity cost of labor:**  Perhaps the cruellest cost is researcher time.  Addgene estimates reagents ≈$155 plasmid (2018 dollars) ([blog.addgene.org](https://blog.addgene.org/the-time-and-cost-required-to-make-a-plasmid#:~:text=Doench%20also%20calculated%20the%20cost,dollars%20to%20over%20a%20hundred)) and assumes an equivalent “value” in scientist time ([blog.addgene.org](https://blog.addgene.org/the-time-and-cost-required-to-make-a-plasmid#:~:text=Finally%2C%20this%20doesn%E2%80%99t%20account%20for,the%20%24155%20spent%20on%20reagents)).  Even if a grad student costs $30/hr, 5 h is $150.  In effect, each DIY plasmid “costs” on the order of **$300+** (reagents + labor).  If gene assembly takes a PhD student a week instead of doing real experiments, that delays productivity tremendously.  (By one reckoning, Addgene saved the community ~8.8 million scientisths hours by sharing plasmids ([blog.addgene.org](https://blog.addgene.org/the-time-and-cost-required-to-make-a-plasmid#:~:text=depositors%20have%20helped%20us%20save,almost%20exactly%201000%20calendar%20years)).)  

In short, cloning failures are common and frustrating, and labs routinely spend many man-hours “just getting clones.”  This pervasive pain – repeated troubleshooting cycles and wasted student/postdoc time – is exactly what an automated service aims to eliminate.  

# Outsourcing Threshold (When Would Labs Switch?)  
There is no published survey explicitly asking “at what price would you stop DIY cloning?”; we infer from costs.  A rough rule of thumb is: **if the outsource price (plus sequencing) undercuts the DIY reagent+labor cost, labs will consider it.**  Using Addgene’s numbers as a guide, DIY consumables run ~$155 (2018) and effective labor ~$150 ([blog.addgene.org](https://blog.addgene.org/the-time-and-cost-required-to-make-a-plasmid#:~:text=Doench%20also%20calculated%20the%20cost,dollars%20to%20over%20a%20hundred)) ([blog.addgene.org](https://blog.addgene.org/the-time-and-cost-required-to-make-a-plasmid#:~:text=Finally%2C%20this%20doesn%E2%80%99t%20account%20for,the%20%24155%20spent%20on%20reagents)).  Thus a fully-verified plasmid effectively costs $\sim$300.  Therefore, an external service offering a completed plasmid for **well below $150–200** (with rapid turnaround) would be economically attractive.  Our Stage 1 target ($30/gene) is far below that break-even, suggesting most labs would leap at it.  

 Anecdotally, repositories like Addgene note that for large needs “it’s almost always faster to order” than clone in-house ([blog.addgene.org](https://blog.addgene.org/the-time-and-cost-required-to-make-a-plasmid#:~:text=you%20from%20having%20to%20make,faster%20to%20order%20from%20us)) – which implies cost parity or advantage.  In practice, if our service could deliver clones (including sequence QC) for **$50–100 each**, labs that currently DIY would likely outsource many projects.  At higher prices (e.g. >$150), only labs with severe time constraints might switch; otherwise they’ll stick to cutting enzymes in the hood.  In short, offering plasmids at a price **comparable to or below the DIY consumable+labor cost** (substantially below current ~$150–200 reagent-only) is key.  No formal survey is available, but industry consensus is that once external assembly is cheaper *and* faster, the DIY crowd will migrate.
