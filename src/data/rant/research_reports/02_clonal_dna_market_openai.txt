# 1. Market size (clonal gene synthesis only)  
The synthetic DNA market is large (estimated ~$2.3 B in 2022 ([www.globenewswire.com](https://www.globenewswire.com/news-release/2023/06/08/2684725/0/en/Gene-Synthesis-Market-Global-Market-Report-2023.html#:~:text=The%20global%20gene%20synthesis%20market,earned%20by%20entities%20by%20providing))) but mostly oligos/short fragments.  Clonal (sequence-verified) gene synthesis is a subset.  One market analysis pegs **clonal DNA synthesis** at about **$1.06 B in 2024**, growing ~17.5% CAGR ([www.congruencemarketinsights.com](https://www.congruencemarketinsights.com/report/clonal-dna-synthesis-market#:~:text=The%20Global%20Clonal%20DNA%20Synthesis,growth%20is%20driven%20by%20escalating)).  As context, Twist (a leading gene-synthesis provider) shipped 558k clonal genes in FY2022 and 634k in FY2023 ([www.sec.gov](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1581280/000162828023039767/twst-20230930.htm#:~:text=%E2%80%A2revenue%20growth%20of%2020,synthetic%20genes%20and%20DNA%20libraries)).  Roughly speaking, industry-wide clonal gene orders are on the order of 10^5–10^6 genes per year (high single‐digit millions in extreme forecasts), implying overall revenues in the low‐to-mid $10^2 M range (a few percent of total gene synthesis).  (By comparison, the broad gene synthesis market was ~$2.8 B in 2023 ([www.globenewswire.com](https://www.globenewswire.com/news-release/2023/06/08/2684725/0/en/Gene-Synthesis-Market-Global-Market-Report-2023.html#:~:text=The%20global%20gene%20synthesis%20market,earned%20by%20entities%20by%20providing)).)

# 3. Labor cost in clonal synthesis  
Clonal gene production remains labor-intensive.  Roughly half of the per-gene cost comes from wet-lab steps (transformation, colony picking, miniprep, sequencing) rather than chemistry ([blog.addgene.org](https://blog.addgene.org/the-time-and-cost-required-to-make-a-plasmid#:~:text=increased%2050,in%20reagents%20and%20consumables%20today)).  For example, an Addgene analysis found ~\$155 in reagents per plasmid and assumed a **similar \$155** in labor time ([blog.addgene.org](https://blog.addgene.org/the-time-and-cost-required-to-make-a-plasmid#:~:text=increased%2050,in%20reagents%20and%20consumables%20today)), i.e. ~50% labor.  Even automated systems require hands-on interventions for clone verification (e.g. picking colonies for sequencing).  In contrast, oligo synthesis chemistry dominates cost, allowing price declines.  No public gene-synthesis provider breakdown exists, but this suggests **labor is ~40–60% of clonal-gene cost**.  (Given ~$100 total consumables per gene, labor would add another $50–100 if done manually ([blog.addgene.org](https://blog.addgene.org/the-time-and-cost-required-to-make-a-plasmid#:~:text=increased%2050,in%20reagents%20and%20consumables%20today)).)

# 4. Volume trends (orders vs revenue)  
Clonal gene orders (by count) are growing briskly, but often less so than revenues.  For example, Twist’s 10-K shows genes shipped **558k→634k** (↑14%) in FY 2022→2023, while synthetic-gene revenue grew **20%** ([www.sec.gov](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1581280/000162828023039767/twst-20230930.htm#:~:text=%E2%80%A2revenue%20growth%20of%2020,synthetic%20genes%20and%20DNA%20libraries)) – implying higher spend per gene.  This suggests **no falling prices**; indeed, Twist’s average selling price rose slightly.  In the broader market, gene-synthesis revenues have risen ~20%/yr, roughly matching or outpacing volume growth ([www.globenewswire.com](https://www.globenewswire.com/news-release/2023/06/08/2684725/0/en/Gene-Synthesis-Market-Global-Market-Report-2023.html#:~:text=The%20global%20gene%20synthesis%20market,earned%20by%20entities%20by%20providing)) ([www.sec.gov](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1581280/000162828023039767/twst-20230930.htm#:~:text=%E2%80%A2revenue%20growth%20of%2020,synthetic%20genes%20and%20DNA%20libraries)).  (By contrast, one might expect “price compression” if volumes far exceeded revenue growth – which is not observed.)  Overall, gene synthesis demand is increasing (e.g. large variant libraries for protein engineering), but providers maintain prices via added features (longer constructs, QC) rather than cutting them.  

# 5. “Twist won’t drop prices” – why?  
Twist launched its gene service in 2016 at about \$0.09/bp (≈\$90 per 1 kb) ([www.twistbioscience.com](https://www.twistbioscience.com/products/genes/gene-synthesis?tab=clonal-genes#:~:text=Twist%20Bioscience%E2%80%99s%20high%20precision%2C%20silicon,and%20accurate%20DNA%20synthesis%20at)) and still charges **\$0.09–0.10 per bp** (~\$125 for a 1.4 kb gene) today ([www.twistbioscience.com](https://www.twistbioscience.com/products/genes/gene-synthesis?tab=clonal-genes#:~:text=Twist%20Bioscience%E2%80%99s%20high%20precision%2C%20silicon,and%20accurate%20DNA%20synthesis%20at)).  Prices haven’t plummeted like oligos for several reasons: every clonal gene must be cloned into a plasmid and NGS-verified, which incurs fixed labor and materials costs.  These include enzymes, vectors, competent cells and sequencing – expenses that have not fallen dramatically.  Moreover, sequence complexity can dramatically reduce yield; automation and ML (used by Twist and Elegen) improve yields but add capital and R&D cost.  As noted above, labor (manual QC steps) remains ~50% of cost ([blog.addgene.org](https://blog.addgene.org/the-time-and-cost-required-to-make-a-plasmid#:~:text=increased%2050,in%20reagents%20and%20consumables%20today)).  In short, unlike oligos where synthesis is purely chemical, clonal genes involve multi-step workflows that are only partly automatable.  Even as robotics reduce some labor, the **overhead of assembly and validation** keeps prices from collapsing.  Indeed, Twist’s gross margins on gene products have stayed ~40–50% ([www.sec.gov](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1581280/000162828024003051/twst-20231231.htm#:~:text=We%20have%20grown%20rapidly%20and,achieve%20profitability%20will%20depend%20heavily)) ([www.sec.gov](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1581280/000162828023039767/twst-20230930.htm#:~:text=%E2%80%A2revenue%20growth%20of%2020,synthetic%20genes%20and%20DNA%20libraries)), indicating value capture rather than purely cost-plus pricing.  

# 6. Customer segments  
Clonal gene synthesis is used by all sectors of biotech R&D.  Twist’s customer mix (Q4 2023) was ~55% healthcare (biotech/pharma) and ~19% academic research (with the rest in industrial/agri & other fields) ([www.sec.gov](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1581280/000162828024003051/twst-20231231.htm#:~:text=Academic%20research%20%20,)).  Similarly, GenScript and other providers service large pharma/biotech for drug R&D (often bulk orders of libraries) and academic labs (typically smaller orders of individual genes/plasmids).  News and conference anecdotes also highlight new demand from AI-driven protein companies (which design novel proteins and order many genes for testing) – these fall under the biotech category.  Typical order sizes vary widely: academics often order a few genes (0.5–3 kb each), whereas consortium projects or biotech slide decks may order hundreds to thousands of variants (omnibus fragment libraries or high-throughput cloning).  In all cases, vector delivery (clones ready-to-use) commands a premium.
