# 1. Addgene – Repository of Plasmids  
Addgene – a non-profit plasmid repository – has been growing steadily.  By mid‐2018 it had shipped its **1,000,002^nd** plasmid ([blog.addgene.org](https://blog.addgene.org/celebrating-one-million-plasmids-shared#:~:text=Can%20you%20imagine%20how%20much,That)).  As of 2023 Addgene has distributed roughly 2 million total materials ([blog.addgene.org](https://blog.addgene.org/twenty-years-of-sharing-science#:~:text=Our%20first%20request%20was%20on,8%2C900%20institutions%20across%20111%20countries)).  In 2022 it shipped about **165,000** plasmids ([blog.addgene.org](https://blog.addgene.org/2022-and-2023-reflections-and-plans#:~:text=Last%20year%2C%20we%20distributed%20165%2C000,Everything%20in%20this)), and 2023 saw **166,247** plasmids (188,729 total materials) ([blog.addgene.org](https://blog.addgene.org/reflections-and-looking-ahead-2024#:~:text=In%202023%2C%20Addgene%20distributed%20188%2C729,from%2037%20different%20countries%2C%20into)).  (This suggests Addgene’s annual plasmid shipments have plateaued in the ~150–170K range recently.)  Addgene’s model is nonprofit: labs deposit plasmids for free, and Addgene charges requestors a fee (covering QC sequencing, storage, etc).  Academic orders are now ~$85–89 USD per plasmid ([www.addgene.org](https://www.addgene.org/news/2023-price-change/#:~:text=,%24180%20USD)) ([help.addgene.org](https://help.addgene.org/hc/en-us/articles/206133505-Where-can-I-find-pricing-information#:~:text=Price%20Per%20Plasmid%20%20,%24585%20USD)) (it was $75–85 prior to 2023 ([www.addgene.org](https://www.addgene.org/news/2023-price-change/#:~:text=,%24180%20USD))); industry users pay several-fold more (e.g. $220–231 ([www.addgene.org](https://www.addgene.org/news/2023-price-change/#:~:text=,%24180%20USD)) ([help.addgene.org](https://help.addgene.org/hc/en-us/articles/206133505-Where-can-I-find-pricing-information#:~:text=Price%20Per%20Plasmid%20%20,%24585%20USD))).  Addgene also sells DNA preps ($105–110 academic ([www.addgene.org](https://www.addgene.org/news/2023-price-change/#:~:text=,%24180%20USD)) ([help.addgene.org](https://help.addgene.org/hc/en-us/articles/206133505-Where-can-I-find-pricing-information#:~:text=Price%20Per%20Cloning%20Grade%20,%24585%20USD))), viral prep services, and multi‐plasmid kits or libraries.  For example, a plate of 96 plasmid clones costs on the order of **$2,400** ([dnasu.org](https://dnasu.org/DNASU/CloneCost.do#:~:text=Price%20per%20Clone%20%20,Quote%20available%20upon%20request)), and Gibson/Golden-Gate enzyme kits run ~$200–250 per set.  

Addgene’s revenue (all reinvested in operations) comes mainly from these fees.  At ∼150–160K plasmids/year × ~$85, plus viral/lab kits, it likely approaches ~$15–20M USD/year (across both academic and industry pricing tiers).  (Exact non-profit financials are not published, but its pricing updates and volume suggest this order of magnitude.)  

**Popular categories:**  A large fraction of Addgene deposits are expression and cloning vectors and genome engineering tools.  In recent years ~30–40K CRISPR/Cas9 plasmids have been deposited; 27,000 CRISPR plasmids were shipped in 2022 ([blog.addgene.org](https://blog.addgene.org/2022-and-2023-reflections-and-plans#:~:text=Last%20year%2C%20we%20distributed%20165%2C000,Everything%20in%20this)).  Other frequently requested types include *fluorescent reporter plasmids*, viral vectors (lentivirus, AAV), and commonly used vectors (T7 expression, Gateway, etc).  For instance, the single most‐used part in their registry (RBS BBa_B0034) has >5400 requests ([blog.igem.org](https://blog.igem.org/blog/2023/6/28/celebrating-20-years-of-standard-biological-parts#:~:text=As%20one%20of%20the%20most,RBS%20sequences%20influence%20gene%20expression)).  Addgene also sells curated kits – e.g. Golden Gate/MoClo parts collections (hundreds of standard modules ([www.addgene.org](https://www.addgene.org/kits/patron-moclo/#:~:text=This%20modular%20cloning%20,two%20linkers%20are%20also%20included))). 

# 2. iGEM Registry of Standard Biological Parts  
The iGEM Registry is a large collection of user-contributed parts (~20 years old).  It contains on the order of **tens of thousands** of parts (published iGEM data implies >15,000+ core parts exist, and many more subparts).  Each year ~3,000 teams upload new parts (via the annual “Give” phase).  However, **most parts are uncharacterized or theoretical**: only parts included in the official annual Distribution kit (hundreds of sequence-verified parts chosen from medal-winning projects ([blog.igem.org](https://blog.igem.org/blog/2023/4/19/a-look-back-on-the-history-of-the-igem-distribution#:~:text=,the%20iGEM%20Registry%E2%80%99s%20sample%20repository))) are guaranteed to “work.”  Anecdotally, only a small subset of Registry parts are reliably used by multiple teams; many parts have no usage record.  For example, the most reused BioBrick (BBa_B0034 RBS) has ~5,000 uses ([blog.igem.org](https://blog.igem.org/blog/2023/6/28/celebrating-20-years-of-standard-biological-parts#:~:text=As%20one%20of%20the%20most,RBS%20sequences%20influence%20gene%20expression)), whereas thousands of parts have never been included in the physical distribution.  

Usage *statistics* are hard to find formally.  iGEM publishes highlights (e.g. the Distributions grew from ~200–300 parts in 2004 to >1700 by 2008 ([blog.igem.org](https://blog.igem.org/blog/2023/4/19/a-look-back-on-the-history-of-the-igem-distribution#:~:text=,the%20iGEM%20Registry%E2%80%99s%20sample%20repository))).  The current Distribution kit (2023) has “hundreds” of parts covering 20 years of iGEM innovation ([blog.igem.org](https://blog.igem.org/blog/2023/4/19/a-look-back-on-the-history-of-the-igem-distribution#:~:text=DNA%20Day%20,teams%20all%20around%20the%20world)).  But beyond that, usage varies widely: popular “core” parts (constitutive promoters, lac/Z/RFP cassettes, origins, etc.) are reused often, while most parts sit idle.  

**Assembly standards:**  The original iGEM standard was BioBrick (prefix/suffix part format, RFC10).  In recent years Golden Gate/MoClo (Type IIS) and Gibson methods have become common in the field.  iGEM has officially moved towards MoClo-compatible parts (the newer “Composite Parts” can be Golden-Gate assembled) and even uses the SEVA vector architecture in some contexts ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12703808/#:~:text=plasmid%20vectors,research%20and%20reducing%20design%20errors)).  Many iGEM kits now rely on standardized Golden Gate parts (MoClo overhangs) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12703808/#:~:text=Later%20advances%20in%20standardized%20DNA,and%20the%20many%20in)).  In practice, legacy BioBricks remain widely available (e.g. iGEM’s BBa parts), but modern teams increasingly use Golden Gate/MoClo format parts for multi-gene assembly.  

# 3. Commercial Part Kits & Toolkits  
**Golden Gate/MoClo kits:** Major suppliers of Golden-Gate (Type IIS) assembly kits include NEB, ThermoFisher, and Addgene. For example, New England Biolabs offers Golden Gate enzyme mixes (e.g. NEBridge kits using BsaI or BsmBI) on the order of **$200–300** for a kit (50–100 reactions).  (As a benchmark, NEB’s Gibson Assembly kit is ~$236 for 20 rxns ([www.neb.com](https://www.neb.com/en/products/e5510-gibson-assembly-cloning-kit#:~:text=Catalog%20,%24236.00)), and Golden Gate kits are comparable.)  ThermoFisher sells “Gibson” and other seamless assembly kits via the GeneArt brand (typically tens to hundreds of dollars per kit).  

**MoClo part collections:** Addgene and academic groups sell MoClo “part kits” (standardized libraries).  For example, Addgene’s MoClo Plant Parts Kit (#1000000047) contains 95 parts (promoters, tags, reporters, etc.) in a 96-well plate ([www.addgene.org](https://www.addgene.org/kits/patron-moclo/#:~:text=This%20modular%20cloning%20,two%20linkers%20are%20also%20included)).  Such kits (96-well, glycerol stock plates) usually ship on dry ice and sell for several hundred to a few thousand dollars.  (DNASU, a similar repository, lists a 96-plate of clones at **$2400** ([dnasu.org](https://dnasu.org/DNASU/CloneCost.do#:~:text=Price%20per%20Clone%20%20,Quote%20available%20upon%20request)) as a reference.)  

**Expression vector kits:** Many companies sell pre-made expression vector kits.  For bacterial expression, NEB’s “pET” vectors, Takara/Clontech entry vectors, or Promega’s systems are common.  Gateway® cloning kits (Invitrogen/ThermoFisher) provide a variety of destination vectors for mammalian, yeast, plant, and bacterial expression (kits ~$200–500 for dozens of clones).  Other examples: NEB sells pET-His-tag cloning vectors; OriGene and GenScript sell curated ORF libraries for human/mammalian genes (sets of dozens to thousands of cDNA clones, often $1000s).  Addgene also offers small “kits” of plasmids (e.g. toolboxes published by labs) on a per-plate basis.  

**Who sells what:**  - **Addgene** sells thousands of plasmids individually (at $65–85 apiece ([www.addgene.org](https://www.addgene.org/news/2023-price-change/#:~:text=,%24180%20USD))) and has a dedicated “kits” section for plasmid libraries.  - **NEB** sells enzyme master mixes (Gibson/MoClo sets) and some vector kits.  - **ThermoFisher/Invitrogen** sells Gibson kits, Gateway kits, and cloning vectors.  - **BioBricks Foundation** is not a seller but an open registry.  - **IDT/GenScript/Twisted** primarily do DNA synthesis, not part kits.  

**Pricing and sizes:**  - Individual plasmids: ~$65–85 each (Addgene academic price ([www.addgene.org](https://www.addgene.org/news/2023-price-change/#:~:text=,%24180%20USD))).  - Golden Gate/Gibson kits: $\sim$200–300 for 50–100 reactions.  - 96-well clone plates: ~$2000–3000 (DNASU quotes $2400 ([dnasu.org](https://dnasu.org/DNASU/CloneCost.do#:~:text=Price%20per%20Clone%20%20,Quote%20available%20upon%20request))).  - Large ORF collections (100s–1000s of DNAs): often custom-quote, typically thousands to tens of thousands of dollars.  

# 4. Open-Source vs. Commercial Parts  
The landscape is mixed.  **Freely available parts:**  Many academic parts are made available under MTAs (usually for non-commercial research).  Addgene indicates that roughly 90% of recent COVID-19 plasmids (initially academic-only) have been opened to commercial use ([blog.addgene.org](https://blog.addgene.org/covid-19-addgene-plasmid-sharing-industry#:~:text=Realizing%20this%20limitation%2C%20we%20worked,profit%20setting)).  Similarly, iGEM parts are public for academic sharing.  However, **commercial restrictions:**  Many parts (especially high-value or patented sequences) carry MTAs that limit industrial use.  Traditional Material Transfer Agreements (MTA) often forbid commercial R&D, and negotiating terms can be laborious.  

Roughly speaking, *most standard parts are “open” to academics*, but a minority have restrictions (especially if drawn from a company or patent pool).  The actual fraction is hard to quantify, but Addgene’s efforts suggest progress in reducing barriers for industry users.  

**Licensing issues:**  Many parts (e.g. fluorescent proteins, Cas enzymes, proprietary promoters) are patented or have “research use only” licenses.  Using them commercially requires licensing arrangements.  Some repositories (e.g. DNASU, Addgene) handle MTAs to allow non-profit use by default.  A truly unrestricted, commercial-use-permissive library (e.g. CC0 sequences) is rare.  

**Demand for open library:**  There is clear community interest in open-source parts.  Synthetic biologists and startups often chafe under MTAs and seek “plug-and-play” libraries.  Initiatives like the Registry, BioBricks, and efforts like SEVA (Standard European Vector Architecture) underline demand for freely reusable parts ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12703808/#:~:text=plasmid%20vectors,research%20and%20reducing%20design%20errors)).  Many users would welcome a “problem-free” parts library (no MTAs, well-characterized, quality-checked) even if they pay a premium, especially in industry settings.  The COVID-19 response, where >90% of plasmids became industry-usable in weeks ([blog.addgene.org](https://blog.addgene.org/covid-19-addgene-plasmid-sharing-industry#:~:text=Realizing%20this%20limitation%2C%20we%20worked,profit%20setting)), shows both willingness and demand when barriers are removed.  

# 5. Market Size Estimate  
The standardized parts/kit market is modest relative to the overall life-science market.  Major players: Addgene distributes ~150K plasmids/year (current revenue ~$15–20M/year).  NEB and others sell assembly kits (perhaps collectively a few $M/year).  If we include commercial ORF/clone libraries (e.g. Human ORFeome, Gateway sets), that might add tens of $M globally.  Altogether, a broad “parts & kits” market (reagents for genetic assembly) is likely on the order of **$10^7–10^8 USD per year** worldwide – i.e., tens of millions, not billions.  (By contrast, the global synthetic biology market is expected to be in the low billions; gene synthesis alone was projected ~$2–4B by mid-2020s.)  

For example, one industry report values the **plasmid kits market** at ~$680M in 2024, growing to ~$1.2B by 2032 ([www.24lifesciences.com](https://www.24lifesciences.com/plasmid-kits-market-873#:~:text=Plasmid%20Kits%20Market%20Outlook%202025,by%202032%2C%20exhibiting%20a%20CAGR)).  However, this includes all cloning/extraction kits (enzymes, reagents) broadly, not just premade part libraries.  The niche of pre-fabricated standardized DNA parts is only a slice of that.  In short, the **addressable market for off-the-shelf parts libraries/kits is relatively small** – likely on the order of $10–50M/year today.  It may grow with biotech expansion, but even then it is dwarfed by total DNA synthesis and sequencing markets.  

# 6. Gap: Curated Verified Parts  
There **is** a niche opportunity for high-quality, validated part libraries.  Community sentiment (and recent scholarship ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12703808/#:~:text=plasmid%20vectors,research%20and%20reducing%20design%20errors))) stresses the value of standardization (SEVA vectors, MoClo) and quality control.  Addgene’s practice (sequencing and QC of all plasmids) is a key part of its service.  But academic deposits vary in quality, and often no functional data is provided.  

Many companies end up revalidating parts by doing their own sequencing and tests.  A “curated, sequence-verified, ready-to-use” library (with bench-tested performance metrics) would save time for labs and companies.  Some specialized libraries exist (e.g. SEVA list of broad-host-range vectors, Biobricks.com collects only fully sequenced parts), but no one has built a *comprehensive* commercial catalog of thousands of vetted parts.  

**Is there a market?** Likely a small, but non-zero one.  Synthetic biology companies, CROs or pharma may pay for “guaranteed” parts to avoid QC headaches.  For example, custom DNA synthesis services indirectly fill this (clients order exactly the sequence they want).  However, an open-curated library (like a better Addgene) could be a differentiator: it would appeal as a convenience and trust-building tool (and marketing).  As long as pricing covers costs (likely low margin), it can act more as a *loss-leader/differentiator* than a big revenue stream. 

**In summary:** The standardized parts and toolkit market is modest.  Addgene dominates the academic side, while small molecular-biology companies serve the rest.  A quality-controlled, open library could find some demand (especially from industry labs tired of MTAs), but should be seen more as a strategic asset than a large profit generator.
