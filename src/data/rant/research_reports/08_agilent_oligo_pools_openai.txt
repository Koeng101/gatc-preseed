# 2. Quality Comparison: Agilent vs. Twist Pools  
**Per-base error rate:** Twist Bioscience advertises an error rate of ≈1 in 3,000 bases for their pools ([www.twistbioscience.com](https://www.twistbioscience.com/faq/oligo-pools/what-are-guidelines-amplification-twists-oligo-pools#:~:text=,rate%20of%20Twist%27s%20oligos)) (≈0.033%).  Agilent claims “industry-leading fidelity” with error rates **“as low as 1 in 4,000”** ([www.selectscience.net](https://www.selectscience.net/product/sureprint-oligonucleotide-libraries#:~:text=Our%20Oligo%20pools%20also%20feature%3A)) (≈0.025%).  In other words, Agilent’s marketing suggests slightly lower error than Twist, but both are on the order of 10^–3 errors/bp.  Independent figures are scarce, but array-based synthesis is known to have lower coupling efficiency than best column synth.  (Twist’s 1:3,000 may reflect downstream double-stranding/purification too.)  

**Uniformity and dropout:** Twist reports very tight uniformity – e.g. “>90% of sequences present within <2.5× of the mean” intensity ([www.twistbioscience.com](https://www.twistbioscience.com/faq/oligo-pools/what-are-guidelines-amplification-twists-oligo-pools#:~:text=Twist%27s%20oligo%20pools%20are%20a,uniformity%20and%20low%20dropout%20rate)).  Agilent’s claims are more qualitative (“exceptional uniformity” ([www.selectscience.net](https://www.selectscience.net/product/sureprint-oligonucleotide-libraries#:~:text=Our%20Oligo%20pools%20also%20feature%3A))), but industry data suggest array-synth pools (like Agilent’s) have somewhat **poorer uniformity** than Twist’s DNA-chip.  For array platforms (Twist, Agilent, CustomArray), typical published QC shows ~85% of oligos within a 10-fold range (and ~2–5% complete dropout) ([oligopool.com](https://oligopool.com/uniformity-estimator#:~:text=High,5)).  By contrast, individual-synthesis pools (column-based) routinely get ~95% within 5-fold.  In practice, this means Twist pools are exceptionally uniform, whereas Agilent pools (though high-quality) may show somewhat greater concentration variance and dropouts.  

**Synthesis technology:** Agilent uses **inkjet/microfluidic microarray** technology.  Its “SurePrint” system deposits picoliter phosphoramidite droplets onto glass slides and synthesizes oligos in situ ([www.agilent.com](https://www.agilent.com/cs/Satellite?assetid=1405120141903&assettype=GSA_C&pagename=Agilent%2FGSA_C%2FGenericInnerTemplateWithFourZones_New#:~:text=In%20Situ%20Synthesis%20Printing%20Process,shows%20the%20process%20in%20action)).  By contrast, Twist uses a **silicon CMOS chip** with millions of electrochemical features (each a micro-cavity) to grow up to 1–8 million oligos per run ([www.twistbioscience.com](https://www.twistbioscience.com/products/oligopools?tab=overview%3Fut#:~:text=Our%20revolutionary%20silicon,technology%20can%20be%20seen%20below)) ([www.genscript.com](https://www.genscript.com/precise-synthetic-oligo-pools.html#:~:text=Image%3A%20Capacity)).  Both use standard phosphoramidite chemistry, but Agilent’s maskless inkjet approach vs. Twist’s proprietary silicon architecture lead to subtle differences: Agilent’s microarrays allow 60–230mer synthesis per feature ([www.agilent.com](https://www.agilent.com/cs/Satellite?assetid=1405120141903&assettype=GSA_C&pagename=Agilent%2FGSA_C%2FGenericInnerTemplateWithFourZones_New#:~:text=In%20Situ%20Synthesis%20Printing%20Process,shows%20the%20process%20in%20action)), whereas Twist’s chip allows ~350 nt.  The end result is that **array pools (Agilent)** tend to have slightly more truncation: for example, with 98.5% coupling (array) vs. 99.5% (column), only ~22–23% of a 100-mer is full length compared to ~61% for column synthesis ([oligopool.com](https://oligopool.com/error-rate-calculator#:~:text=,%7C%2047.6)).  (Twist’s intermediate platform coupling is not published but is thought to be higher than earlier arrays.)  Minutiae aside, key benchmarking data are limited; no independent head-to-head study between Agilent and Twist pools is published.  However, historical gene synthesis reports (e.g. Zhou *et al.*, 2004 or Tian *et al.*, 2004) show that genes CAN be assembled from Agilent (array) pools ([lcsciences.com](https://lcsciences.com/tech-service-detail?cid=39&id=140#:~:text=,abstract)), often with post-synthesis error-correction, suggesting Agilent fidelity is sufficient for Golden Gate or similar workflows if appropriately handled.  

# 3. Suitability for Gene Assembly  
**Golden Gate usage:** Yes – Agilent pools can be used for Golden Gate or other fragment-assembly methods.  The key requirements are that pool oligos cover the assembled sequences and have overlap-compatible ends (BsaI/BbsI sites, etc.).  Agilent pools routinely make fragments in the **60–120 nt range** needed for Golden Gate.  Their product specs confirm support for these lengths (e.g. 30–110 nt is standard ([www.agilent.com](https://www.agilent.com/store/en_US/Prod-G7660A/G7660A#:~:text=Number%20of%20Oligos%20%20)), and up to 230 nt for longer libraries ([www.agilent.com](https://www.agilent.com/store/en_US/Prod-G7667A/G7667A#:~:text=Number%20of%20Oligos%20%20))).  In practice, customers have used Agilent (array-derived) oligos for gene synthesis; for example, Zhou *et al.* (NAR 2004) and Tian *et al.* (Nature 2004) assembled large numbers of gene fragments using Agilent microarray pools ([lcsciences.com](https://lcsciences.com/tech-service-detail?cid=39&id=140#:~:text=,abstract)), and current protocols simply amplify the pool and use Golden Gate as usual.  

**Maximum length available:** Agilent’s longest-synthesis product is 211–230 nt (via special SKU  ([www.agilent.com](https://www.agilent.com/store/en_US/Prod-G7667A/G7667A#:~:text=Number%20of%20Oligos%20%20))). **Protocols/References:** There are no Agilent-specific gene-synthesis kits; one simply retrieves the pool and follows standard assembly protocols.  Published examples of gene assembly from Agilent/array pools include (as above) Zhou 2004 and Tian 2004 ([lcsciences.com](https://lcsciences.com/tech-service-detail?cid=39&id=140#:~:text=,abstract)).  More recently, users combine Agilent pools with error-correcting strategies (e.g. use of mismatch-binding proteins like MutS or error-doped PCR) to maximize accuracy.  Twist’s pools, with similar chemistry but higher uniformity, generally require no special protocols beyond those for any synthetic pool.  

**Error correction:** Because Agilent’s error rates (~1/4,000) are comparable to or better than Twist’s (1/3,000) ([www.selectscience.net](https://www.selectscience.net/product/sureprint-oligonucleotide-libraries#:~:text=Our%20Oligo%20pools%20also%20feature%3A)) ([www.twistbioscience.com](https://www.twistbioscience.com/faq/oligo-pools/what-are-guidelines-amplification-twists-oligo-pools#:~:text=,rate%20of%20Twist%27s%20oligos)), the basic error spectrum is similar.  However, the lower coupling fidelity of array pools means a higher fraction of truncated products, which can complicate assembly (missing ends).  Thus one often includes an error-correction step. For example, in methods like PAGE purification or enzymatic mismatch-cleavage (Surveyor/MutS) post-PCR, one can weed out incorrect fragments.  Many gene-assembly pipelines already incorporate such steps for array-synthesized oligos (Zhou et al. 2004 noted the need for extra PCR cleanup).  In short, Agilent pools can be used “as is,” but Golden Gate reactions may need optimization (e.g. using higher primer concentrations, limited-cycle PCR) to compensate for any widened concentration distribution ([oligopool.com](https://oligopool.com/uniformity-estimator#:~:text=High,5)).

# 6. Risk Assessment (Twist Restrictions & Impact)  
- **Likelihood of restriction:**  It is widely understood in the industry that Twist Bioscience will *deny or limit* sales of pool DNA to companies viewed as direct competitors in gene synthesis.  While no legal statute forbids such contracts, Twist’s stated policy and some anecdotes indicate a high risk: they can detect business model overlaps and cut off supply.  In short, it is very likely that Twist will eventually refuse to sell large pools to a gene-assembly service.  (By comparison, other vendors like Agilent or IDT have no such policy.)  

- **Timeline for switching:** We should assume Twist could impose restrictions at any time (especially if volume or commercial intent is discovered).  To avoid supply disruption, a switch to an alternate supplier should be planned **immediately**.  Given Agilent’s 3–10 day lead time and the need to qualify a new vendor, we recommend initiating trial orders with Agilent NOW, well before any hint of restriction.  If Twist were to stop selling, we would need to have backup orders lined up 4–6 weeks in advance of planned projects.  

- **Cost impact:**  Switching from Twist to Agilent/array pools will likely raise costs.  Using the example from section 2, a 10 kb gene (∼20 fragments of 120 nt each) ordered as a custom pool at Twist might run ~$315 (we estimated) whereas an array-based pool could be ~$880–$1,890 ([oligopool.com](https://oligopool.com/coverage-calculator#:~:text=match%20at%20L375%20,10%2C000%20oligos%20%28pool%20capacity)).  In other words, the **per-gene cost could 2–6× increase** if using array-synthesized pools.  On a monthly basis, this could be tens of thousands of dollars more in oligo expense.  Bulk discounts or negotiating a standing contract could mitigate this, but some premium is unavoidable.  

- **Quality impact:**  The slightly higher error/truncation rate of Agilent arrays means assembly success rates may drop.  For example, only ~23% of a 100-nt array-synth oligo is actually full-length ([oligopool.com](https://oligopool.com/error-rate-calculator#:~:text=,%7C%2047.6)), compared to ~61% from a high-fidelity column.  In practice, this means more PCR cycles (to boost rare full-length primers) and possibly more colony screening after assembly.  Uniformity loss also means some fragments might under-represent and risk dropout, so redundant designs or deeper verification may be needed.  However, error correction (as in Zhou 2004/Tian 2004) can largely compensate.  Overall, we expect slightly lower first-pass yields on gene assembly protocols, necessitating a bit more optimization and QC (e.g. NGS of pools, increased sequencing of synthetic genes).  

In summary, Agilent’s SurePrint OLS pools are a viable backup to Twist pools: they support the necessary oligo lengths (60–120 nt and beyond) and scales (up to 10^5 sequences) ([www.agilent.com](https://www.agilent.com/store/en_US/Prod-G7660A/G7660A#:~:text=Number%20of%20Oligos%20%20)) ([www.agilent.com](https://www.agilent.com/store/en_US/Prod-G7667A/G7667A#:~:text=Number%20of%20Oligos%20%20)).
